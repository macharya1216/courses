Foundational concepts organized by topic

The Basics

Opportunity Cost

Opportunity cost refers to the value of the next best alternative that is forgone when making a decision. For example, if you choose to spend time studying instead of working a part-time job, the opportunity cost is the income you could have earned during that time.

Examples

Example 1: A simple, real-world example that illustrates the concept.

Thinking in Margins

Thinking in margins means considering the additional benefits of an action versus the additional costs.

Examples

Example 1: For instance, when deciding whether to produce one more unit of a product, a company evaluates if the revenue from that unit exceeds the cost of producing it

Marginal Cost

Marginal cost is the cost of producing one additional unit of a good or service.

Examples

Example 1:If a factory produces 100 cars at a cost of $1,000,000 and the cost to produce one more car is $10,000, the marginal cost of that car is $10,000.

Market Equilibrium

Market equilibrium occurs when the quantity supplied equals the quantity demanded at a certain price level.

Examples

Example 1:If a new smartphone is priced at $500, and consumers are willing to buy exactly 100,000 units at that price, the market is in equilibrium.

Sunk Cost

Sunk cost refers to money that has already been spent and cannot be recovered.

Examples

Example 1:If a company has spent $1 million on a project that is failing, that amount is a sunk cost; the company should not let it influence future investment decisions.
